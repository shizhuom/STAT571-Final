# Predicting Blood Cell Types from Single-Cell RNA-Seq: A Comparative Study of Classical and Foundation-Model Approaches

**STAT 571 — Modern Data Mining, Final Project**

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) now routinely profiles the transcriptomes of thousands of individual cells, but assigning a biological identity ("cell type") to each cell remains a central analytical bottleneck. Manual annotation is subjective and laborious, while automated annotation is an ideal proving ground for modern data-mining techniques: the feature space is high-dimensional (thousands of genes), the classes are imbalanced, and the ground-truth labels are noisy. The goal of this project is to systematically compare supervised, unsupervised, and foundation-model approaches to this classification problem, using the widely studied **PBMC 3k** dataset (2,700 peripheral blood mononuclear cells, 1,838 highly variable genes, 8 expert-assigned cell types).

Three families of methods are evaluated on an identical stratified 80/20 train-test split. **(1) Regularized logistic regression** (L2 and L1) is trained on both the full HVG expression matrix and a 50-component PCA projection; L1 coefficients double as a sparse marker-gene selector, and L2 coefficients are used to recover the top-ranked genes per cell type. **(2) A multilayer perceptron** (three hidden layers with BatchNorm + Dropout, inspired by the ACTINN/scNym architectures) is trained with weighted cross-entropy and a `WeightedRandomSampler` to counter class imbalance. **(3) Unsupervised clustering** — K-means with elbow/silhouette model selection, and Leiden across a resolution sweep — is evaluated against the Louvain ground truth using ARI and NMI, to test whether structure in gene expression alone recovers biological identity. As a stretch extension, **(4) pretrained foundation-model embeddings** from scGPT (512-d, transformer pretrained on 33M cells) and Geneformer (256/512-d, BERT-style rank-value encoding, pretrained on ~30M cells) are extracted in a zero-shot setting and fed into the same downstream LR/MLP classifiers, isolating the value of large-scale self-supervised pretraining.

All methods are compared on accuracy, macro-F1, and per-class performance, with clustering methods additionally scored by ARI/NMI. Five-fold cross-validation and confusion-matrix analysis quantify stability and per-cell-type error patterns. The expected outcome is that regularized LR on HVGs will already achieve high accuracy (>0.90), the MLP will yield a modest improvement, and clustering will recover coarse cell-type groupings but confuse closely related lymphoid subsets (CD4 vs CD8 T cells). Foundation-model embeddings are hypothesized to narrow that gap by encoding cross-dataset biological context that PCA cannot. The broader takeaway is methodological: for a well-characterized dataset of this size, parsimonious linear models remain a strong baseline, and the marginal gains from deep learning must be justified against their cost in interpretability and compute.
